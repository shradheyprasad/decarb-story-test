India is the world's most populous country and the fourth largest economy. It is also the third biggest producer of electricity and carbon emitter. As it continues to expand its energy access and industrial base, the demand for reliable, affordable, and clean electricity will grow rapidly. Recognizing this challenge, India has committed to achieving carbon neutrality by 2070.

In collaboration with the Prayas (Energy Group) and Sylvan Energy Analytics, the project advances India's decarbonization efforts through open-source datasets, models, and analyses that strengthen planning and capacity building at both national and state levels.

GridPath-India, built using the open-source GridPath power systems modeling platform, is an open-source electricity system planning and operations model with a spatial resolution of 34 load zones, one for each state and union territory, and one for Bhutan.

India in 2020

Coal Dominates Today

India's electricity system in 2020

Fossil fuels generate 74.9% of total electricity demand. Coal alone accounts for 235 GW -- 60.3% of installed capacity -- and 994 TWh of energy generation, or 71.4% of the total. This is the starting point from which India's decarbonization must begin.

India in 2020

Generation by State

Regional disparities across India

State-wise electricity generation reveals deep regional variation. Some states rely almost entirely on coal while others have growing shares of renewables -- reflecting differences in resource endowments, industrial demand, and policy priorities across India's 34 load zones.

The Transition

Renewables Take Over

Cost-optimal capacity deployment 2020-2050

Solar PV, wind, and battery storage dominate new infrastructure investments across all scenarios. VRE capacity grows from 72 GW in 2020 to 421 GW in 2030 -- exceeding India's official non-fossil target of 500 GW -- and reaches ~2,902 GW by 2050.

4 / 9

The Transition

Storage Is the Backbone

Battery and pumped hydro expand dramatically

As variable renewables grow, storage becomes the backbone of grid reliability. Battery storage and pumped hydro scale up dramatically across all scenarios -- enabling the grid to balance supply and demand even as coal retreats.

5 / 9

The Transition

Coal Becomes Backup

How dispatch shifts as renewables grow

As low variable-cost renewables expand, coal's role fundamentally changes -- shifting from primary energy generator to a reliability backup. By 2050, coal-related variable costs fall sharply as the fleet runs fewer hours each year.

6 / 9

The Economics

Technology Costs Drive the Story

How cost assumptions shape outcomes

Technology costs for solar, wind, and battery storage strongly influence overall system outcomes. Lower cost trajectories reduce total system costs and enable deeper emissions reductions -- making clean energy not just the climate-rational choice, but the economic one.

7 / 9

The Economics

Bills Stay Manageable

Levelized cost per MWh across all scenarios

Despite growing total system costs driven by electricity demand growth, real electricity costs per MWh remain lower than 2020 levels across all future investment periods. The clean energy transition does not mean higher bills.

Key Findings

Three Takeaways for Policymakers

1. Electricity system costs decline over time, even while meeting ambitious long-term carbon targets. Our results show that, across a range of technology and demand assumptions, overall system costs fall relative to current levels, including under conservative cost scenarios.

2. Cost-optimal clean energy deployment exceeds the official near-term 2030 target. Under conservative assumptions, renewables supply the majority of electricity, supported by large-scale batteries, while achieving meaningful emissions reductions.

3. Climate policies or clean energy targets are essential in the long-term to meet ambitious carbon mitigation and net-zero goals. Climate policies or clean energy targets are essential to meet a 90% carbon emissions reduction goal, which will make India's carbon neutrality goal by 2070 increasingly attainable.
